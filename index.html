The Challenge

Digitising 25 Million Cultural Treasures

There exists an ever-growing dependence on technology and the digital world throughout society, and as such, there exists an expectation of digital access to all aspects of life, including history (Lareina Yee, 2024). To not be left behind and maintain relevance and connection, the World Museum is seeking to digitise its approximately 25 million, and growing, scientific specimens and artefacts obtained from across the globe over five years (Mandakovic, 2023).

Pillar 1: Vision Capture

Advanced Digital Preservation

Next: AI Analysis

Multi-Technology Approach

Portable 3D scanning for complex objects, high-resolution photography for detailed capture, multi-angle imaging for large items, and specialized flatbed/film scanners for historical documents and media.

Preservation-First Design

Overhead camera rigs minimize handling of fragile items, archival sleeves prevent direct contact, portable solutions reduce object movement, and high-quality capture ensures single-session success.
The vision capture equipment will cost ~$39,000 AUD. This investment is expected to deliver a strong ROI by enabling high quality scans. We are going with a scan once, preserve forever approach, utilising state of the art equipment. This approach minimises handling, reduces energy use, and ensures the digital preservation of the collection for future research, access, and repatriation.

Environmental Impact

Low-power LED lighting

Minimises electricity usage during scanning (Department of Energy, 2025).

Carbon-neutral cloud storage

Data stored and hosted on Microsoft Azure - Carbon neutral since 2012, aiming for carbon negative by 2030 (Microsoft Azure, 2025).

On-site Scanning

Use of portable scanners to avoid unnecessary transport (reduces vewhicle and logisitc emissions).

Challenges & Mitigations

Fragile objects (glass plates, old prints)

Overhead cameras, archival sleeves to prevent direct contact.

Large or heavy objects

Portable 3D scanners for no contact imaging.

Inconsistent labels or handwritten data

High-resolution images will allow specially trained AI to extract text in later phases.
Pillar 2: AI/ML Analysis

Automated Recognition: Zero in on Every Artefact

Train machine learning models to spot objects and pull out details from every image. Labels, tags, handwritten notes, the lot. AI does the heavy lifting, turning raw images into searchable data.

Direct-To-Database: No More Manual Typing

AI-extracted info (what it is, where it's from, condition...) pipes straight into the digital register. For tricky or valuable pieces, humans review and approve, ensuring accuracy without the bottleneck.

Fast, Accurate, and Scalable

Slash manual entry by up to 60%. Enriched digital records mean better research, easier access, and a big boost for digitisation funding.
ROI Impact: 40-60% reduction in manual labor for data entry, enhanced accuracy through standardized processing, and increased accessibility potential for heritage grants and monetization.

How It Rolls Out

Start with what's already labeled. Let the AI learn, then scale up. Every new object gets scanned, recognised, and logged in real time. Experts double-check the edge cases.

Comprehensive Asset Management

Real-Time Tracking

25+ million unique RFID chips enable automatic object identification. Long-range readers process 1,200 tags per second, with specialized cryogenic labels for frozen specimens at -80°C.

Enhanced Security

Automated alerts for unauthorized movement, secure encrypted data transmission, integrated movement logging, and real-time monitoring across all storage zones and access points.
Investment: $6.88M AUD for complete RFID infrastructure including 25M+ UHF labels, 380 cryogenic labels, printing systems, and 350+ fixed readers with handheld units for security checks.

Detailed Implementation Timeline

5-Year Strategic Implementation Plan

Our proposed digitisation solution will take effect over a five-year period, and below is a Gantt chart that shows the progress for the digitisation of different objects and other key components of the solution.

Year 1 - Laying the Foundations

The first year will involve the commencement of the vision capture process and the printing and attaching of RFID tags for the following object types:

Small Dry Objects
Foundation phase
Pinned Objects
Foundation phase
Paper-Based Objects
Foundation phase
Audio Visual Objects
Foundation phase

With the data obtained throughout the first year, the training of AI models on high-quality images will begin. The primary focus of the first year is on objects that do not require special handling.

Year 2 - Further Digitisation

The second year will continue with the vision capture process for Small Dry Objects and finish the vision capture process for Paper Based Objects and Audio Visual Objects. The digitisation of the Pinned Objects will be enhanced by establishing an extra station to handle these objects.

Additionally, AI/ML will be utilised to begin extracting data from scanned tags and labels through OCR and image analysis, and further, RFID-based tracking on all scanned objects will commence.

Year 3 - The Heavy Lifting

The third year will begin the vision capture process on Large/Heavy Objects and Liquid Preserved Objects. During this year, AI/ML will be expanded to handle degraded text and unusual label formats, and RFID readers will be used to track and integrate movement logs for objects.

Year 4 - The Finishing Touches

The fourth year will see the finalisation of vision capture for Liquid Preserved Objects and the commencement of vision capture for Frozen Tissue Samples, which require extra care when handling. At this stage, AI/ML will be fully integrated into the Populated Asset Register (Database), and community input will be obtained for validation and cultural enrichment purposes.

Year 5 - Final Steps & Optimisation

In the fifth and final year, vision capture will be completed for the Frozen Tissue Samples and any remaining objects, such that a searchable, public-facing database can be launched. Further quality assurance will be undertaken throughout this final year to ensure that repatriation-ready digital records exist. Finally, the museum will launch a full digital asset register and tracking system.
